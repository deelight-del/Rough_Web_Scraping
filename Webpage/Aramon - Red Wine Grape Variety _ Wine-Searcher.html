Aramon Grapes





Aramon is a black-skinned wine grape variety from the Languedoc-Roussillon wine region in southern France. Since the middle of the 19th Century it has been widely used, in part because of its resistance to mildew and disease, and also because it can produce a very high yield. However, the quality of wine made from Aramon leaves little to be desired and the variety is slowly fading into obscurity.


From the 1850s to 1950s, vast swathes of Aramon vines were planted, predominantly in (but not restricted to) the Hérault area of the Languedoc. These produced large crops used to make undistinguished, pale red wine.


The variety allowed people to continue to work in the wine industry at a time when traditional wine regions and varieties were suffering from the onslaught of vine pests brought from the Americas – most notably phylloxera.


As the rural population was driven to the cities by the impact of agricultural change and the industrial revolution, Aramon provided the industrial north of France with large volumes of cheap wine. As Aramon wines are typically very pale in color, they were often blended with Alicante Bouschet, a purpose-bred teinturier variety.


The plantings of Aramon began to decline when French territories in Algeria began producing robust red wines, particularly Carignan, at low cost, negating the need for Aramon wines. Aramon has also been grown in Algeria and Argentina, but is probably known better as the parent of a rootstock variety AxR1, which was grafted to Vitis vinifera vines to provide resistance to phylloxera. This rootstock turned out to have less resistance to the aphid than expected and large areas of vines in California had to be replanted when it was found that they were infested and not able to survive the pest.


Synonyms include: Brom, Eramoul.
